Architecting an Engagement Engine

A detailed look at how I led a foundational research initiative to diagnose a failing feature, and then orchestrated a multi-quarter roadmap to transform it from a simple tool into a core driver of user retention and platform value.

Role: Lead Product Designer Outcome: +21% Conversion Lift, New Retention Loops Skills: Foundational Research, Interaction Design, Product Strategy

1. The Setup: A Leaky Funnel & a Crisis of Trust

In early 2022, the Heart Rate Monitor (BPM) was a strategic bet on organic growth and user engagement. The business objective was two-fold: create a "wow" moment for **first-time users** to drive acquisition, and build a valuable tool that **repeat users** would integrate into their health routines, increasing long-term retention.

However, analytics painted a grim picture. The feature was underperforming dramatically. Our primary business metrics were failing:

  • First-Time User Conversion: A catastrophic ~38% of users were dropping off before successfully completing their first measurement.
  • Long-Term Retention: Churn analysis showed that even users who were successful once rarely came back. The feature wasn't "sticking."

As the lead product designer, I was tasked with diagnosing this strategic failure and leading the cross-functional effort to turn it around.

2. Discovery: Uncovering the 'Why'

2.1 Guerilla Research: Hearing the User's Voice

The data showed *what* was happening, but not *why*. I initiated a quick guerilla research sprint, conducting informal usability tests with a diverse user group. The unfiltered feedback was raw and illuminating. Instead of abstract personas, we were hit with direct, powerful quotes.

Instruction Mismatch
"I saw the video, but I still had to ask my son what to do. The words are difficult and I don't know which camera to use."
The "Phone Flip" Behavior
"I kept flipping my phone over to see if my finger was in the right place. It’s hard to do without looking at the back!"
Trust & Accuracy Concerns
"My blood pressure is high, but the app said 'Healthy'. I don't trust the reading if it gets this wrong."

2.2 Analyzing the V1 Experience

These qualitative insights drove me to conduct a formal audit of the V1 experience. I identified five core failures that directly contributed to the drop-off and lack of trust. I presented these findings to stakeholders not as surface-level UI issues, but as deep, systemic flaws in the user experience.

Problem: Static illustrations failed to guide users on where to place their finger, causing immediate failure before the process even began.

Problem: The UI gave zero feedback on signal quality. Users couldn't tell if they were pressing too hard or too soft, leading to anxiety.

Problem: Once the scan started, the UI was a passive countdown. Users had no confirmation that their pulse was being detected correctly, leading to questions like "Is it working?"

Problem: The "Unable to detect!" error is a dead end. It blames the user without providing any actionable advice on how to fix the problem.

Problem: The results screen provided a number but no context or value, making the feature feel like a gimmick. This was the root of the low retention.

2.3 Synthesizing with a User Journey Map

I consolidated all these findings into an Emotional Journey Map. This artifact was crucial for stakeholder alignment, as it translated abstract data and user quotes into a clear narrative of declining trust. It pinpointed the exact moments where our experience was failing and where we had the biggest opportunities for intervention.

But the problem ran deeper than just first-time users. To understand our retention failure, I also mapped the journey for a returning user. This revealed a completely broken loop that provided no value over time, dooming the feature to be a one-time-use gimmick.

3. Defining the Design Goals

With a deep, evidence-backed understanding of the problem, I established three clear, measurable design goals that would guide our entire redesign effort:

1

Achieve 'First-Try' Success

Overhaul the onboarding and measurement experience to be foolproof, intuitive, and anxiety-free for all users, regardless of tech-savviness.

2

Build Unshakeable Trust

Provide transparent, real-time feedback and proactively educate users to eliminate confusion and build confidence in the result.

3

Create a Habit Loop

Transform the results screen from a dead end into an actionable, insightful launchpad that provides clear value and encourages repeat usage.

4.1 Solving for 'First-Try' Success: An Onboarding Deep-Dive

Fixing the 38% drop-off required a multi-point attack on the onboarding journey. I broke the problem down into distinct intervention points and explored solutions for each.

Crafting the First Impression

Our research showed users were both curious and skeptical. I chose a video intervention not just to instruct, but to build excitement and establish credibility from the very first second.

Video Storyboard Concepts

Approach: A direct, technical animation showing the heart, the phone, and the measurement process.

Pros: Clearly explained the "how."
Cons: Felt a bit cold and impersonal; didn't build an emotional connection.

Approach: Show a relatable person feeling happy and healthy while using the app.

Pros: Built strong emotional resonance and trust.
Cons: Less effective at explaining the technical process itself.

Decision: We combined the best of both. The final video is fast-paced, human-centric, and crucially includes a "5M+ Readings Recorded" social proof element to instantly build trust and credibility, directly addressing a key user concern.

Bridging Intention and Action

There is a critical moment between the video and the user placing their finger. The design of this screen needed to guide them seamlessly into the measurement.

Pre-Measurement Screen Concepts

Approach: A full-screen camera view with a "pull-up" drawer for instructions.

Pros: Maximized the camera view for easy finger placement.
Cons: Hiding instructions required an extra user action and wasn't discoverable for less tech-savvy users.

Approach: A circular camera view to act as a "target," with a small, persistent video guide.

Pros: The target metaphor felt intuitive. Persistent instructions were always visible.
Cons: The smaller view made it slightly harder to place the finger correctly. Through testing, we found a full-screen view with always-visible text was the most effective compromise (our final design).

Decision: We shipped a hybrid solution. A full-screen camera view for easy finger placement, but with clear, always-visible persistent video guide. This provided the best of both worlds: maximum viewable area and upfront instructions.

Designing a Proactive Safety Net

My analysis showed that V1 handled errors passively—it only told the user *after* they had already failed. I explored a more proactive approach to prevent failure before it happens.

Error Handling Approaches

Concept: After a measurement fails, show a screen listing all possible reasons.

Pros: Simple to implement. Better than no information at all.
Cons: This is a "post-mortem" approach. The user still has to guess which of the multiple reasons caused their specific failure. It's unhelpful and places the cognitive load on the user.

Concept: Provide real-time, contextual feedback *during* the measurement to prevent failure in the first place.

Pros: This "active" approach is far more empowering. A subtle "Motion Detected!" banner allows the user to correct their behavior mid-scan, saving them from a full 30-second failure.
Decision: We shipped the Active Handling system. It was a clear win for user experience, turning a moment of potential frustration into a moment of successful guidance.

4.2 Building Trust During Measurement

My research clearly showed that the 30-second scan was an "anxiety-inducing black box." To solve the "phone flip" and build user confidence, I designed a multi-sensory feedback system to make the invisible process, visible and tangible.

Making the Invisible, Visible

One of the primary sources of distrust was that users couldn't see anything happening. My first exploration focused on providing clear visual proof that the system was working.

Visual Feedback Concepts

Approach: Make the live BPM reading the hero of the screen. The primary focus is on the user's heart rate number, which updates in real-time.

Pros: Gave users the immediate data they wanted.
Cons: In testing, users found the constantly fluctuating number distracting and even anxiety-inducing. They didn't have a clear sense of how far along they were in the process.

Approach: Make the progress circle the primary visual element, with the BPM number as secondary information.

Pros: Clearly communicated how much time was left, which users found calming and reassuring.
Cons: Hiding the live BPM data felt like a step backward from a transparency perspective.

Decision: We shipped a balanced, hybrid solution. It features both the clear progress circle to manage expectations, and the live BPM number for transparency. We also added the live PPG graph at the bottom as a definitive "proof of life" for the system.

Feeling the Heartbeat

To solve the "phone flip" directly, I explored non-visual cues that would allow users to feel the app working without needing to look at the screen.

The Final Sensory Solution: I worked with engineering to implement a subtle, rhythmic "lub-dub" haptic pulse that syncs with the user's detected heartbeat. This allowed users to *feel* the measurement happening, providing constant reassurance without needing to look at the screen. This multi-sensory approach—combining visual proof with physical feedback—was incredibly effective at eliminating the "phone flip" and building user confidence.

4.3 Designing an Actionable Result

Fixing the measurement was half the battle. To drive retention and fulfill our second business goal, we had to answer the user's "So what?" question. I redesigned the result screen to transform it from a dead-end data point into a rich, contextual, and actionable health dashboard.

From a Number to an Answer

The first step was to make the single reading immediately valuable and trustworthy, directly addressing the confusion between BPM and BP that we found in our research.

Contextual Result Concepts

Problem: The original screen showed a number in a vacuum. It answered "what" but not "so what?", creating confusion and distrust, especially for users who mistook it for a Blood Pressure reading.

Solution: I introduced several layers of context:

  • Clear Visual Ranges: Replaced the vague "Healthy" label with a colored bar showing Low, Healthy, and High zones.
  • Contextual Tagging: Added a prompt for users to tag their state (e.g., Resting, After Exercise), which then provided a more accurate interpretation.
  • Proactive Education: Included a small, explicit disclaimer: "This is a heart rate reading, not a blood pressure measurement."

Building the Habit Loop

To encourage repeat usage, the result screen needed to become a gateway to deeper, longitudinal insights.

Retention-Focused Concepts

Approach: Display a historical graph of past readings directly on the result screen.

Pros: Gave users a reason to measure again—to see how their data changes over time. This directly creates a habit loop.

Cons: A graph alone is still just data. Our HRV research showed that users wanted interpretation, not just visualization.

Decision: The final screen became a true engagement hub. We shipped a design that included:

  • The Trend Graph as the centerpiece for retention.
  • "Learn More" Modals to educate users on advanced metrics like HRV, addressing findings from our user calls.
  • Actionable CTAs ("Check Health Risk," "Share Report") to guide users deeper into the platform's ecosystem, creating tangible value from their data.

5. Impact & Learnings

Positioning Eka as a Trustworthy Health Partner

This initiative was more than a redesign; it was a strategic turnaround. By grounding our decisions in deep user research and data, we transformed a failing feature into a core pillar of the Eka.Care experience, fundamentally changing user perception from a 'gimmick' to a trusted health tool.

FTU Conversion
+21%
From 62% to over 75%
Power User Retention (W4)
~80%
For users with 35+ measurements

Key Learnings

  • Trust is the Ultimate Metric. A 'wow' feature that fails is worse than no feature at all. My biggest takeaway was that for health tech, flawless execution and building user trust are more important than novelty. The confusion between BPM and BP was a critical blind spot that our research uncovered.
  • Design is the Synthesizer. My role went far beyond pixels. I was responsible for connecting the dots between quantitative analytics, qualitative feedback, and business strategy. Evangelizing the 'why' behind the data and creating artifacts like the journey map were essential for aligning the entire team.
  • Retention is a System, Not a Screen. Fixing the UI wasn't enough. Lasting impact came from designing the *entire system* around retention—from contextual results and trends to intelligent notifications and clear educational content.
