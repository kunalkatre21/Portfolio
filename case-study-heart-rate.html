From Confusion to Conversion: How I Lifted Feature Success by 30%

A deep dive into how I used guerilla research and a key interaction pivot to diagnose a failing feature funnel, rebuilding it to drive user trust and measurable business impact.

Role: Lead Product Designer Company: Eka.Care Outcome: +30% Conversion for First-Time Users Skills: User Research, Funnel Analysis, Interaction Design, Prototyping

1. The Setup: A Failing Strategic Asset

The Heart Rate Monitor (BPM) was a key pillar of our user acquisition strategy, designed to be a free, high-value "wow" moment to differentiate Eka.Care and drive initial app downloads. Its success was directly tied to our top-of-funnel growth targets. However, the data told a different story. It was a leaking bucket. Our analytics showed a massive drop-off, with overall conversion for the feature struggling at around 64%. For the crucial first-time user cohort, the numbers were even worse, sometimes dipping below 60%.

The business problem was clear: a key strategic asset was underperforming and creating a poor first impression. My task was not just to fix a UI, but to diagnose and reverse a business failure.

2. The Diagnosis: Leading a Cross-Functional Investigation

I brought my initial hypothesis—that the issue was a fundamental crisis of trust and clarity—to my partners in Product and Engineering. We agreed that before investing development time, we needed to validate the root cause. I took the lead on a multi-pronged diagnostic process.

Guerilla Research & User Observations

I initiated and led guerilla research sessions with 8+ users, deliberately selecting a mix of personas, from tech-savvy software engineers to less tech-literate security guards. Observing users interact with the app provided immediate, unfiltered, and damning evidence. The core of the problem wasn't a minor usability issue; users were fundamentally confused.

Mapping the Failure Points

To synthesize these qualitative findings and present a clear case to stakeholders, I created a user journey map. This artifact became the centerpiece of our investigation, allowing the entire team to visualize exactly where the experience was breaking down.

The "Aha!" Moment: Synthesizing the Insights

The combined research provided three critical insights that I shared with the team:

  1. Instructional Failure: Static illustrations were ineffective. Users couldn't connect the abstract drawing to their physical phone.
  2. Feedback Void: The system gave poor feedback, causing users to break the measurement process by flipping their phone over to check finger placement.
  3. The Trust Deficit: A lack of clarity led to a lack of trust. Users perceived the feature as a gimmick and abandoned it at the first sign of friction.

3. The Pivot: From Better Illustrations to a Better Medium

Armed with clear evidence, I prototyped several solutions. Our initial explorations into clearer static illustrations proved to be only a marginal improvement in testing. This led me to the key strategic pivot: the problem wasn't the content of the instruction, but the medium itself.

I created a simple video prototype and presented my findings and the new direction to the broader team. By showing session recordings of users failing with the old UI alongside the clarity of the video concept, I secured buy-in for the additional effort required to implement a video-based onboarding.

Architecting the New Experience

The solution was a complete overhaul, focusing on clarity, trust, and designing for the entire experience—not just the happy path.

Key improvements included:

  • Video Onboarding: A short, looping video showing a real hand performing the action, which proved unambiguous in testing.
  • Multi-Sensory Feedback (PPG Graph & Haptics): To solve the feedback void, I collaborated with engineering on a two-pronged solution. We introduced a live PPG graph so users could see the measurement in real-time. Critically, we also added a subtle, rhythmic haptic pulse that mimicked a heartbeat, allowing users to feel that the measurement was active without needing to look at the screen. This combination of visual and physical feedback was pivotal in building user trust.
  • Designing for Failure & Constraints: The original feature was built quickly to meet a deadline, resulting in significant design debt. We addressed this by redesigning error states to be empathetic and instructional. To ship quickly, we made a pragmatic trade-off to use a static list of "Fun Facts" during the measurement, logging a dynamic version as a V2 improvement.

4. The Payoff: Measurable Turnaround and Validated Hypotheses

The redesigned flow was rolled out, and we monitored the results closely. A key concern was that adding a video might increase load times, so we set "Time to Interactive" as a counter-metric. By optimizing the video asset, we ensured there was no significant regression, confirming our fix didn't introduce a new problem.

The results were a clear validation of our research-led strategy.

The Impact in Numbers:

  • The conversion rate for the critical First-Time User cohort increased from a baseline of ~58% to a consistent 74.5%, a relative improvement of nearly 30%. This directly validated our hypothesis that the instructional medium was the core problem.
  • The success rate for users who re-attempted after a failure increased by 23%, proving that redesigning the error state from a dead-end to an encouraging loop was a critical, high-leverage change.

5. Reflection & What's Next

This project was a defining moment in my growth as a product designer. It taught me the importance of leading with data, validating with qualitative insights, and framing design decisions in the context of business objectives.

Organizational Impact

Beyond my personal growth, this project had a lasting impact on our team's process. The success of using guerilla research to quickly diagnose and solve a major business problem built more trust in the design process, leading to design being brought into strategic conversations earlier. The video onboarding component we created also became a new pattern in our design system, later adopted for two other complex features.

Future Opportunities

Fixing the initial conversion was Phase 1. This success unlocked Phase 2: focusing on long-term retention. My follow-up proposal detailed a 'Streaks & Insights' feature, giving users a compelling reason to measure their heart rate regularly. Furthermore, a key insight from our research was the user desire to share results with family or doctors. This highlighted a major opportunity to connect our BPM feature to our 'Family Health' and 'Telemedicine' platform pillars, creating a pipeline for future high-value work.
